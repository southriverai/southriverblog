# The desk

## **Roy**

Roy had always considered himself a minimalism enthusiast. Neither he nor his wife tolerated any clutter around the house. At the office he abided strictly by the clean desk policy. Yet the orthogonal lines and lack of features in the stone desk protruding out of the desert floor in front of him made him deeply uncomfortable. 

It was not supposed to be there; they were supposed to be the first on Mars. They had landed two months ago with much fanfare. On a live television broadcast Roy had given a speech and they had all read letters written by schoolchildren from across the globe.

Somehow someone had gotten there before them. They had brought a desk and chairs. The desk was three by two meters with a rather thick top. Four chairs were arranged around it, three on one side and one facing them. Outside of the subtly rounded edges not a single curve could be found in the whole arrangement.The outer chairs were oriented slightly inwards in a way that Roy found slightly disorganized. He thought briefly about rectifying it but the whole thing was made out of what seemed to be polished basalt. The imagined weight of just the chairs was intimidating. 

Roy realized that his misgivings about the furniture were probably a displaced emotion that originated from the lady waiting patiently beside it.

Roy had never had a hallucination before. He was sure being slightly unimaginative was a requirement for any deepspace mission commander. He forced himself to take another look at the woman. She looked vaguely Asian but perhaps that was more the blouse and pants than anything else. Her attire seemed far more comfortable than his except that it was at least minus forty out here.

She cast a brief inviting smile in his direction and pointed at her head.

“Your helmet, you can take it off if you want.” Her voice came through muffled like speaking though a bottle, but its temperature and pitch were feminine in character. Every machine he had operated on the surface had made a deep rumbling sound like mechanical moving glaciers.

Roy operated his suit controls to check for hypoxia or anything else that could cause him to see things but everything seemed within bounds. “How is your pulse doing?” The woman asked. But Roy decided to ignore her a little longer. He was still operating under the assumption that this was some sort of fever dream even though it did not feel like it. He had some alternate theories but some sort of delusion was preferable and more likely.

He tried his radio again. “Mary, Steven,  you guys there?” Event though the radios were dualband he waited for a moment to give them time to respond. “Anyone…?” He tried again. Nothing but static. 

He had made a mistake. A compounding of mistakes. He should not be out here on his own, but against mission protocol he had sent the rest of his crew to retrieve a replacement drill while he went looking for a better place to use it. And now he found himself in a situation he might not be able to handle.

Out of options, Roy decided to meet the woman's gaze.

She smiled again. “It’s the metal deposits you are prospecting,” she said.” They are interfering with your radio”

“Don’t worry you are not hallucinating.“ She added a moment later. The statement was of minimal reassurance. Despite his best efforts at calming himself she was not going away. Roy considered that engaging with hallucinations might be risky, but found it too hard to play stum.

“Are you with the Chinese?” He asked her. The people's republic had abandoned their mars program after unrest about its spiraling costs forced the party to reconsider. But there was no other nation on earth that could land anything like this on the planet.

“I think you know I am not. Your heart rate seems to indicate as much.” Roy nodded slowly in response.

“You might want to sit down for what I am about to tell you.” Her open hand pointed towards the chair waiting for him. “Please…”

Roy carefully touched the top of the chair. Testing both the back and the seat for solidity.

“Don’t worry, it's real, it’s been there for months.” The woman tried to reassure him.

Carefully Roy gave the stone construction his weight. It felt real at least.

The woman held out her hand. Was he supposed to shake it? After keeping it hanging there for a moment she withdrew it again and went to stand behind the chair in front of him. From some drawer on her side of the desk she retrieved a piece of paper and put it on the black surface in front of him.

“Dear Dr. Richards” She had changed her tone to something formal “I am here to invite you and the rest of your planet to join the galactic community of civilizations.” Her tone implied capitals at the end of that sentence. For some reason Roy expected her to go on but she was now waiting for his reaction.

Roy did not have any. “I… I am going to need a moment”  He managed.

He remembered there had been a protocol for this. Somewhere sometime long ago before talk of great filters and modern realism about interstellar travel had made the possibility of in person first contact seem a remote one. Training on managing the social dynamics of living together in tight spaces for years on end must have taken its place in the astronaut training program.

“Look, I am sorry… you have the wrong person” Roy stammered. He again looked over the woman in front of him standing on immaculate black pumps in the frozen Martian soil. She looked human. Her chest was moving but what was she breathing?

She walked around the table and sat on the corner closest to him. Dust drifted off her shoes in tiny specs, the material looked like leather but it did not behave like it.

“Not what you thought this was going to be like?” She asked. Her voice was still formal but there was sympathy in there as well.

He forced himself to look her in the eyes. They betrayed nothing of the intellect behind them. They seemed human eyes, they vibrated slightly as tiny saccades moved her gaze around his face and met Roy’s eyes alternatingly. Did real people favor a single eye or was this a normal way of looking someone in the eyes?

“You need to go to the president, or even the United Nations." Roy explained.

The woman carefully shook her head. “From experience we know this process can be rather chaotic, so at the moment I am going one at a time… starting with you.”

Roy looked at the two seats on his left.

“Stephen and Mary will be joining us soon enough.” She said, That should have sounded ominous but somehow it didn’t. Perfect voice control, some part in the back of his brain noted. Not quite enough to land her into the uncanny valley, remarkable nonetheless.

Roy forced his mind back into the here and now. “*Be present, be practical”* he reminded himself. There was something strange about the paper in front of him.  

Dust was everywhere on mars, it settled on the rocks, it stuck to his visor, it settled in the cracks of his suit. But somehow it had touched neither the desk or the paper on it. Like the woman's immaculate pumps it did not want to be near it.

He touched the piece of paper she had put in front of him. And his gloved fingers left big orange smudges on the bottom right corner. No magic then.

*“Welcome”,* the header said.

“Is this a joke?” Roy asked. Not in defiance but as an honest question.

The woman smiled at him. “More of an attempt and normality. As much as me and everything around you is physically real. You must realize that all of it is a fabrication to make this go a bit smoother. The paper doesn’t mean anything, it is your cooperation I am interested in.”

For a moment Roy considered what this being needed his cooperation for. They had travelled between stars. It could take whatever shape it liked. But still it was talking to him, having a conversation, in English.

He reached for the mission patch glued to the right shoulder of his suit. “Tantum prorsum”  it read, *Only forward*. As much as his mind was looking to retreat in the sea of considerations and contingencies piling up in the back of his mind. If he was honest to himself, none of them seemed truly useful right now. As much as precaution and planning had been central to the mission he signed up for, exploration of this kind could never be truly safe.

Roy heaved himself out of the chair and stood beside the desk. He looked the woman in the eyes again. “Can we maybe start over?” He asked.

Roy tried not to second guess this time. If this was some sort of ploy or ruse it might be deserving to succeed. He was equal part scared and curious where this would go.

“By all means,” the woman said.

Roy stepped forward and held out his hand. She grasped the sturdy glove and gave it a gentle shake.

“Robert Patrick” he introduced himself as he would to a congress man “..from earth.” He added belatedly.

“Call me the Gardener.” She said, There were more questions buried into that introduction than it provided answers.

She waited a moment for the implication to settle in and added “I tend to this solar system. I make sure this neck of the galactic woods does not turn into a dark forest”. Yet more questions buried in answers. One thing at a time, Roy decided.

“Garderner” Roy weighed the name in his mouth. “I assume there is more to you… besides this… ‘this shape I am familiar with’” For the first time he smiled back at the woman. Understanding they were now playing a game within the game now. She flashed a smile back at him. But then went back to a more formal visage.

 “I”  She put a strange emphasis on the word. “am a shard of a distributed intelligence that has been monitoring this system for the last half a billion years. There is enough of me here on Mars to get the job done here but, most of me is spread in tiny specs across to Oort Cloud.”

Roy followed her eyes as she looked up for a moment. As far as he could tell there was nothing abnormal to see there but he lingered his gaze at the stars for a moment to catch his breath. She waited for him to come back to her before she continued speaking.

“The *me* standing before you though is a facsimile, an android, like the table and the chairs I was built a few months ago in a remarkably small craft that is inconspicuously parked a few miles away.”

“A machine envoy.” Rot concluded.

“Tragically the most common kind. Mostly homegrown too. Nearly all of my material components were constructed in-system but the original seed came from a factory in orbit around what you would call *Kapteyn’s star*.” She pointed at a point in the distance just above the horizon.

Roy recognized the name but blanked on the distance. He knew it was close though. Maybe a dozen lightyears?

“Who built the factory then?”

“Alas the *what* question is simpler to answer than the *who* question. Already the hands that added to the factory were numerous  but as you will soon discover ideas are everything, and the number of minds that went into the idea of ‘me’ and other gardeners like me are legion. Most of these minds eventually originate in something you would recognize as organic life but not all of them.”

Roy let all of this sink in for a moment. He glanced over to the three seats parked on his side of the desk and decided to try his radio again, whether to call for backup or warn off his colleagues he wasn’t sure. Nothing but static again.

“Does part of *the job* include interfering with my radio?” He asked.

She shook her head. “As I told you before there are deposits in the soil that are messing with the reception part of your duplex uplink. If all of you move the offset slightly you might be able to connect again but it is mainly a signal strength issue.”

Roy went through his radio option and tried to boost the reception amplification to no avail. Then another thought occurred to him. “But could you?”

“I have been monitoring every step in your specific technological development, you will find there is very little I cannot do to your suit, your rover, your rocket or the old Iphone 12 buried in the bottom drawer of your desk back home. Yet I would rather rely on choosing conditions carefully than tampering with your computers.”

Roy considered that statement for a moment before giving up on his radio. Even if it worked he wasn’t fully sure who he would tell what. Even if first contact was not in his job description he was the expedition leader, this was probably the kind of excrement that would roll back uphill to land on his desk anyway. That notion made another thought occur to Roy.

“I am guessing your boss has a broader agenda than horticulture.” He hypothesised.

The Gardener shot him a relieved look. As if she “We”  The Gardener put a strange emphasis on the word. “are interested in your diversity.”

“Perhaps, you should talk to the shipboard AI. We were criticized for leaving most of diversity back on earth”

The Gardener shook her head slightly. “Let me give you an example. As we are speaking the ether around us is abuzz with ideas. Somewhere in the next century or so, your culture will build its first interstellar antenna. My database contains thousands of designs based on many different principles. Based on my observations of your current technology, I can make a pretty educated guess what it is going to look like.”

“But what if I am wrong, what if you build a different design. What…. If it doesn’t look like any of them. This would be something of value, your culture would be galactically famous within a few millennia.”

“So you are interested in our engineering prowess?”

“Novelty. Novelty is the only trade-good that seems worthy of interstellar transfer.”

“And you are here to recruit me to your trade embassy mission.” 

“I think I already did. We are just bargaining on your salary now.” She said, Roy was stunned at the assertion. In his mind. he was not at all convinced he should be helping who or whatever he was meeting.

“I imagine there isn’t any salary.” Roy played for a moment to think.

The Gardener shook her head.

Roy took another moment to understand what the Gardener was suggesting.

“What is it that you want me to do exactly?” Roy made it clear his skepticism was audible.

“It is more suggesting than demanding, I would like to emphasize again that the work I do is the result of a consensus. There is no interstellar invasion fleet out there to impose their will upon the planet. Simply talking to someone for a million years until they agree with you is what an empire looks like on the galactic scale. That said, it would be helpful if you would inform sensible people about my existence one at a time. Perhaps small groups at some point. Attempt to cool tempers when wider awareness eventually hits the 6 o’clock news.” the Gardener explained.

“Cooling tempers” Roy repeated to himself. That was something he was good at.

“I guess you can start with her” The Gardener nodded her head at something behind Roy.

## **Mary**

Mary was holding out a magnetic riveter like it was a gun. The device was designed to bolt down pre-fab components like habs and solar arrays to their in-situ fabricated mars-crete. Some of the components around the muzzle looked mangled. Roy assumed that Mary compromised the safeties in order to turn it into an improvised firearm. She was striding in his direction with Stephen on her tail.

“Roy, get the fuck away from that thing\!” The sound of Mary's voice over the radio sounded extremely harsh. The improvised weapon was probably deadly at point-blank range but Roy expected the accuracy to be atrocious.

“Mary, I want you to meet the Gardener.” Roy tried to calm her down. “We have been…”

From a few meters away Roy had difficulty making out her expression but her stride and voice made no mystery of her mood. “We have been listening to your conversation Roy\! She is not human, that’s what she is.” She is a woman, and pretty one at that. Why do you think that is?” Her shouting voice came through crystal clear on the radio but he could hear a strange muffled echo as well through the densified air around them.

Thankfully Mary had not come alone. “Stephen, what's happening here?” Roy asked the middle aged German scientist coming up behind her.

Thankfully Stephen sounded much calmer. “She has been shouting at me to drive faster all the way here. I had no idea what she was getting up to in the back with the riveter. Although I have to say I do not fully disagree with the sentiment.” He said.

Roy just shook his head in response and went back to the woman wielding the improvised firearm. “If she is not human Mary, what do you think that out of spec powertool is going to do? I don’t think her mind is even in this body.”

“I am not going to shoot it. I am going to nail it to that fucking desk and then we are all going to walk out of here.”

Roy stood there looking puzzled. He wasn’t sure Mary was on board with her own plan, she was still pointing the weapon at the Gardeners chest. To nail her down, through her hands probably, she would have to get a lot closer. A moment passed, and then another. 

Eventually it became clear to Roy that some sort of stalemate had been reached. “Look Mary, I thought about running. I almost did. Give me one scenario in which that would change anything.” He took a brief pause, wondering if his next words were going to make things worse or show Mary that he shared at least some of her perspective. “From what I have seen It’s in our suits, in our computers. I am fairly sure it has complete control over our ascent vehicle.” He wasn’t really convinced that the Gardener would stop them if they tried to leave but he was trying to emphasize the futility of violence.

Mary still had some fight left in her. “We have weeks before our return window closes, I am very confident I can do to the accent vehicle what I did to this tool.”

It was a foolish argument. They might make it into orbit Apollo style but even if they managed to mate with the orbiter above their troubles would redouble from there. 

“There is no way we are managing the Z-pinch without AI, let alone any Computer.” Roy objected

“We destroy all the transmitters and unbox the backup AI from Isolation.” Mary suggested. But she was grasping at straws. Stephen shot Roy a look and mentioned with his hands that he should take it easy. The older man stepped up besides Mary.

“Mary, It flew through interstellar space.” Stephen weight in. His voice sounded tired, no doubt Stephen felt the stress of the situation as well.

“You don’t know that\!” Mary shouted her voice was cracking now. Her determination was either sapping or building to a dangerous climax. “All you know is that it has technology that seems to be better than ours.”

“You think she is man made? Does she look like Elon Musk's idea of a practical joke to you?” Roy asked.

“Fuck yes\! That is exactly what she looks like\!” Mary shouted and walked up even closer to the Gardener, waving the riveter at her face now. Roy was tempted to step between them but by his own logic the improvised weapon was no threat to the Gardener.

“Mary, you are arguing with reality. Is it inconceivable that someone from earth came here before us in secret and left all this.” Stephen argued.  “Conceivable but extremely unlikely.” Stephen lacked Roy's raw people skills but, over the years they had built up considerable rapport. He knew how to argue with her.

“Look at your suits readings.” He added “There is air here, warmth, there is even something blocking the sun's radiation. We could take off our suits and be completely fine. There is no technology on earth that can do this.” 

Mary briefly glanced at the display on her right arm, swaying the weapon off-target. An abandoned part of Roy's brain remembered bootcamp and told this had been the moment to disarm her. The moment passed and her arm tensed up with renewed vigor.

“How are you feeling Mary?” the Gardener asked her.

“Fucking terrified.” Mary overarticulated.

“Would it help if I let you nail me to the desk?” The Gardener put her hand on the black surface as an invitation. “I would not feel a thing.”

Mary’s hand holding the rivetter shook for a moment and then she threw it on the floor. She strode off a dozen meters to a rock poking out of the sand and sat down on it with her back towards the others.

Was she actually throwing a tantrum? Roy wondered. Then she started fiddling with her helmet. Her hand angrily explored the metal oval that connected it to the rest of her suit. It took Roy only a moment to figure out what she was doing and before he could make a single step towards her she had managed to pull the seal. An angry hiss of air equalized the pressure between her and the martian atmosphere. Roy caught his breath for a moment but relaxed when Mary angrily tossed the helmet on the ground beside her and buried her face in her hands.

Both Roy and Stephen felt like they should give her some space but the Gardener walked up to where Mary was sitting and stood beside her and stared at the stormy horizon.

Mary tilted her head slightly to look at the Gardener's shoes in the sand beside her before reburying her hand in her wet hair.

“If It helps anything in many ways I am in the same conundrum as you guys. I woke up here with just programming and a bit of hardware” It said. “I know only what I have been told. I can try to validate that given knowledge against my own observations but, for a machine like me, is it even more impossible to know myself. They could have fitted me with all kinds of blind spots and limitations, there would be nothing I could do about it.” She said

“That does not help at all.” Mary grumbled. 

The Gardener averted its gaze from Mary and looked up at the stars. Freed from meeting or evading its gaze Roy was finally free to observe and question the androids' form more deeply. Did those eyes even see? The Gardener was not really looking up, it was just moving her head trying to get Mary to mimic her. To match her feigned attention. For a moment Mary resisted but eventually Mary also raised her eyes to the stars. “What does it look like to you? The galaxy.” Mary asked. 

“It’s busy, busier than you can imagine. And it's loud, just not in the channels you have been listening to.”

Mary took two breaths before finally voicing the question Roy had been turning over in his mind, too afraid to ask himself. “Is it all machines, or…” Her voice faded as her imagination briefly ran ahead of her.

“The technospheres can be quite big. But most of their creators are still around in one form or another.”

“What do they look like?” Mary did not make it clear which she was actually interested in.

“I think that is for you to find out for yourselves.” Even though there was a rejection in the Gardeners word it did not register in her voice. 

Mary did not fail to pick up on the semantics. “Or you could just tell me.” her suspicion came flooding back. It wasn’t a simple question but also not an impossible one. Did they all look the same? Did they all look like humans with rubber masks? Was earth secretly life seeded? Some other possibilities from an Isaac Azimov novel also came to mind. The air suddenly felt dry in her mouth. Was it even air she was breathing?

“Perhaps she doesn’t know,” Stephen suggested. Possible, but unlikely. If the universe was such a loud place would no one be sharing selfies? Mary's eyes locked with the Gardener’s. So human, but that was the point. She stood up again and the Gardener rose with her.

“Ow, she knows.” Mary said “What is with the sudden close lips?”

“I think she does not want to reveal too much..” Roy explained. “That’s what she told me before, she hopes we form our own ideas, our own technologies.” Diversity for diversities sake, Mary did not believe in galactic wokeness.

“What is the interference in just telling us what our next door neighbors look like?” Mary pressed.

“Roy is correct,” the Gardener said. “I follow a policy of minimal interference. ”

“What part about this shit”  Mary spread her hand showing the gathering around the desk. “is minimal? You could have sent us an email\!”

“Lets get out of here, apparently there is nothing to be learned.”

## **Stephen**

##  

“Minimal is not zero. Minimal could be quite a lot really. ”

“Look, we have 10 to 15 more minutes before Roy's rebreather starts to hit the orange, ours will not be far behind. As this seems set to be a one time performance I suggest we learn what we can.”

“Next time I will send an email.” The Gardener confirmed. Stephen shuddered at the concept. “Chaos does not teach…” Mary muttered under her breath. 

Stephen shot her a look to shut up and Mary started fiddling with her helmet.

Stephen put on his lecturer's voice. Roy and Mary had both heard it before but only when he was speaking about lava flows and wind erosion. “Lets disregard the possibility of overt deception for a moment because you are correct, that would be a bottomless pit. Look at her and the table. If just think about the forces it would take to move her and possibly the desk. I agree with Roy's assessment that there is no brain in the body, it might even lack a powersource. No offence intended … “  Stephen addressed the Gardener.

“None taken.” The Gardener replied.

 Stephen continued “…but the whole thing might just be a puppet operated remotely with only magnetic fields. Just to know that such technology is possible, practical even.” The immediate crisis seemingly abated, the scientist in Stephen finally made it to the surface.

Stephen tried to scratch his beard in thought but his hand clumsily hit his helmet. “So you live out in the oort cloud, and in living memory you have not sent any craft to earth? How do you stay informed?.”

“I watch both inwards and outwards with a vast array of eyes and instruments.” Stephen made eye contact with Roy who returned his look. Was there a whiff of poetry in there? Stephen did not believe in rational unbiased computing which given the current circumstances was both frightening and exciting.

“Eyes like what? Synthetic aperture radar?” Stephen continued his line of questioning even though he knew he would run into a wall at some point.

“That’s as good a model as any.”  The Gardener twisted her head a little bit. Like a schoolteacher warning a clever but mischievous child.

“Stephen you are being led astray” Mary warned him.

Stephen raised a finger and looked at Mary “Why would she lie to us? She could have just held her tung” For a moment Stephen closed his eyes. His lips moved as he did some calculations in his head. Stephen opened his eyes for a second to ask: “At what range can you maintain coherence?” The Gardener just returned him a blank stare and the wisp of a smile. “I am going to assume a single astronomical unit.” Stephen stated and closed his eyes again.

When he opened his eyes again they were shot through with excitement. “Even in the infrared you could have mm level resolution.” A brief pause “Optical frequencies?” He wondered out loud. The question was rhetorical. The Gardener tossed him a smile, this was all the technical specification he was going to get.

Stephens turned around with a wide grin directed at the other two astronauts.

“Ow, I imagine her knowledge about our future is far less interesting that what she knows about our past.”

“What are you saying Stephen?” Roy asked but Mary had inferred the implications. “That she has a historical record of everything that happened under the sun since the Cambrian.” She concluded.

“How many Spartans died at Thermopylae?” Stephen pressed the Gardener.

The Gardener smiled. “If I said three-hundred-and-fifty-two would you believe me?”

Stephen shook his head in disbelief. “She could resolve every historical or prehistoric controversy\! Was Socrates ever real? Who built Great Zimbabwe? The first Clovis crossing, She has records of all of it\!”

“She is not going to hand it over though.” Mary stated. “Our ability to establish and retain or forget fact is a key characteristic of our civilization. If she is truly following a policy of minimal intervention this information is out of our reach.” She had picked an awful time to turn philosophical.

Stephen's eyes darted between them. Finally settling on the Gardener. She had the audacity to look slightly apologetic. “I won’t destroy or hide it. Eventually you will manage to pry it from me.”

“When we come looking for it in the outer solar system.” Mary sighed facing the heavens. As if she could see the web of microsatellites parked beyond the orbit of Pluto. After a moment she looked at Stephen and tilted her head like the Gardener had done moments earlier. “And not before.” She added

Stephen looked very unhappy with this conclusion. “This is preposterous” He said “first you tell us you know the face of Jesus Christ and then won’t tell us what he looked like.”

“I can’t be helped, the existence of the information can be easily inferred unless I pretend to be blind or a recent arrival. Which again are obvious lies in their own rights. But Mary is right, I can’t give it to you.”

“Minimal interference my ass. Simply the knowledge that this information is out there will stifle the will of people to go looking for it. You are damning archaeology, paleontology, history and a bunch of other fields to irrelevance.” Stephen complained.

“Me being here changes everything. I won't deny it. But intentionally not everything all at once.” All the emotions the Gardener displayed were carefully crafted facimeles Roy had concluded. But the apologetic look she displayed now, although perfectly crafted, was the most disingenuous of them all. She had come here knowing that would be the implication.

“I have a consolation prize perhaps.” The Gardener opened a drawer in the desk and pulled out a piece of metallic rock. She walked back from the desk to stand in front of Stephen and pressed the fist sized rock in his hands. It was clearly heavier than he had expected.

“From a comet formed around Kaptyn's star.” The Gardener said.

Stephen's eyes lit up. A physical sample from an interstellar body. It felt sacred, as if his hands would contaminate it, but then again what could a little rubber do that a few million years of martian dust rub had not..

“I picked it off the ground this morning just a few kilometers away.” The Gardener explained. “You could have found it yourself. They are not that hard to find once you know what to look for.” “And witnessed their impact.” Mary interrupted. The Gardener nodded at her briefly.

“I sampled the comet as it drifted into the system. It is what I used to verify the origin of my source material.”

“It won’t mean much but if you work hard you will be able to verify it against the isotope signatures of the other material orbiting Kaptyn's star. And eventually, when you find it, you too can check against my seed to confirm its origin.” Stephen very much doubted he would live long enough to witness it but it was a very pretty gift horse so he would not object.

An anxious beep from Roy's suit brought Stephen out of his revelry.

“Guys, it is time for me to go.” Roys said. 

“I think I am past due,” Mary added. Her suit display had lit up like a Christmas tree of warnings.

“Before we go, there is one more thing I need to ask,” Stephen said. He looked back at the Gardener. “Why here? Why now? Is Mars such an achievement that we now deserve to be in the club?” 

The Gardener shook her head. “Mars is a convenience, it's away from prying eyes and it has a very controllable guest list.“

“I am not about to believe in superintelligent laziness," Stephen objected.

“I think Mary knows.” the Gardener suggested and focused on the woman that was still trying to reboot some of her suits systems. “Mary, what day is today?”

It took Mary a moment to wrestle herself loose from the task she was working on. She shot the Gardener a look then peeked back at her suit display. Then her mouth dropped open. “sonit” She whispered under her breath.

“What’s that?” Stephen asked. But Roy had heard her.

“SONIT” Mary repeated louder now. “Today is the first day of operation of the Sagan observatory for near infrared transits. It's gonna work.” She looked at her wrist computer to inspect the time “It is working” She corrected.

“Working spectacularly well.” The Gardener added “It will find dozens of strong biosignatures in this week alone. The scientific community might have been able to blank on some of the stuff James Webb Telescope threw up. This time it will be undeniable.”

“Ow, Maryland must be hectic right now.”
